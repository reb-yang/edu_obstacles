Financial situation is greatest obstacle faced by Canadian students

2020-09-25

Abstract

Many Canadian students have aspirations for higher levels of education, but do not always believe that it is likely to achieve them. This research studied the obstacles they face in obtaining their desired level of education as reported in the 2016 General Social Survey conducted by Statistics Canada. It found that financial situation was the most common obstacle and the majority of obstacles were reported by women. These findings reiterate the existence of roadblocks in attaining education and the importance for government funding and financial aid to make education accessible for all students.

Introduction

First paragraph: General. Motivational. Majors specific finding.

“Stay in school, kids”. This mantra has been preached to children for generations, instilling in them the idea that education is the key to social mobility, financial security, and a stable future. This holds even more true for post-secondary education; the 2016 Canadian census reported that men and women with a bachelor’s degree earned 47.2% and 58% more, respectively, than their counterparts with only high school diplomas (Statistics Canada). However, while society has made education very desirable, the question of whether or not it is attainable for all students remains. Perhaps unsurprisingly, the most commonly identified obstacle in getting education was the respondent’s financial situation.

Second paragraph: More about what you did and found.

Many students reported a difference in their desired level of education and the level of education they believed they were likely to complete. The disconnect between students’ aspirations and what they believed possible warranted further investigation into what exactly is standing between them and their desired education. After all, since education is such a good thing, shouldn’t students be able to get all the education they want? When asked if they faced obstacles in obtaining education, almost a third of students responded “Yes”. Of those students, 68% identified financial situation as the leading obstacle. It was also found that the majority of respondents who reported facing obstacles were women over men. The breakdown of the specific obstacles faced varied between sexes and age groups as well.

Third paragraph: Outline of the paper. Future work. Weaknesses. Finally, some weaknesses of these findings must be acknowledged. The data used was from a survey that was targeted towards all Canadians, so students are only a subset of the full sample. Consequently, when the data was broken down by the different obstacles and compared between different groups, the sample sizes became very small (n<30). As such, these findings should be taken with a grain of salt and merely serve as the impetus for further discussion and investigation. A survey targeted towards students about the obstacles they face and ways they can be best supported is the logical next step in bridging the gap between students’ educational hopes and realities.

It seems counterintuitive to promote education as a society while still having such high barriers to postsecondary education. The financial burden of postsecondary education may seem like common knowledge at this point, but apparently it has not gotten through to everyone. In 2019, the Ontario provincial government announced cuts of $670 million to OSAP, the Ontario Student Assistance Program. While they justified it through a 10% tuition reduction, student aid packages were reduced by much more, and grants were replaced by repayable loans.

Data

The data used is from the General Social Survey (GSS), a survey conducted annually by Statistics Canada in order to gain insight into Canadians’ social lives, habits, views, and well-being, and usually focuses on a particular aspect each year. This analysis makes use of the 2016 iteration of the GSS, which focused on “Canadians at Work and Home”. The target population was residents of Canada, except for the Yukon, Northwest Territories, and Nunavut, aged 15 and over that were not institutionalized.

Individuals who reported that their main activity was going to school were identified as students and asked specific questions about their educational plans and goals. Non-college certificates, private business programs, trade school programs, and the like were considered together as “Non-college whatever”. Students over the age of 25 were also considered together as one age group.

The variables of interest were the following:

  • likely_future_edu: the level of education the respondent thought they were likely to attain
  • desired_future_edu: the level of education the respondent desired to attain
  • sex
  • age_group
  • obstacles: whether or not the respondent faced obstacles in attaining their desired education
  • obstacles_future_edu: the specific obstacles identified by the respondents

The distinction between likely and desired education by the survey was of particular interest. The following graph compares the percentage of students who would like to attain a certain level to the percentage of students who believe that they will actually accomplish that level. From the graph, it can be seen that Master’s and Ph.D. degrees are not perceived to be attainable, as not all students that desired them believed they were likely to acquire them. Instead, they seem to have resigned themselves to the fact that they will probably “settle” for a Bachelor’s, as 35% of students think they will likely get a Bachelor’s compared to 27% that are actually aiming for them. On the flip side, for lower levels such as college degrees, non-college certificates, and high school diplomas, smaller gaps exist.

The disconnect between students’ aspirations and what they believed possible warranted further investigation into what stands between them and their desired education.

When asked if they faced obstacles in obtaining education, 31% of students responded “Yes”. Financial situation was overwhelmingly identified as the main obstacle students faced, comprising 68% of responses. Other obstacles identified included the length of time the program requires (5%), the inability to get into a program (6%), personal health (5%), and having to care for children (4%).

This is unsurprising given the rising costs of higher education across Canada. According to Statistics Canada, in 2015, 50% of students graduated with student debt. And it doesn’t look like it’s getting more affordable/ The financial burden of postsecondary education is compounded by cuts to government funding for low-income students. In 2019, the Ontario Student Assistant Plan (OSAP) was cut by $670 million, leading to a reduction in grants and forcing students to take on student debt to finance their education.

Breaking down the obstacles faced by gender found that the majority of obstacles were reported by women. In particular, child care had the greatest disproportionate effect. Of those who said caring for children was an obstacle, a whopping 93% of them were women (*See weaknesses)

Discussion

My discussion… main findings of your graph.

Features of your survey…. Non-response… etc. And how these could affect the results….

Since the target population was all Canadians, not specifically students, it seems likely that mature students (over age 25) were overrepresented in the sample. This may have influenced the results as people of different ages have different priorities and challenges in their respective lives. For example, financial situation was an obstacle for 68% of students aged 15-24, but only affected 50% of students aged 55-64. Child care was practically a nonexistent issue for students aged 15-45, but was an obstacle for 21% of 35-44 year olds. However, this can also be seen as a benefit of this survey as it incorporates a portion of the student population that may otherwise be overlooked, instead of focusing on the “typical” student. Of course, this depends on whether the aim is to help younger students. Education may be considered a better investment for younger students than students aged 55-65, for example.
